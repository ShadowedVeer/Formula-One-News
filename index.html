What Is Formula 1

The World's Fastest
Racing Series

Formula 1 is the highest class of international single-seater racing, sanctioned by the FIA and contested since 1950.

The word "formula" refers to the strict set of rules every car must obey — a shared rulebook that turns each team's engineers loose to extract performance from open-wheel, open-cockpit machines that look and behave like nothing else on the planet.

Across a season, drivers and teams race for two prizes: the Drivers' World Championship, awarded to the individual, and the Constructors' Championship, awarded to the team that scores the most points across both of its cars. The calendar circles the globe — from the streets of Monaco to purpose-built temples of speed across five continents.

Every weekend is a fusion of human courage and cutting-edge technology, where a tenth of a second can decide a career.

Anatomy of a Grand Prix

How a Race Weekend Works

A Grand Prix isn't a single event — it's a three-day build-up where every session sets up the next. Order matters, and it runs like this.

01 Fri–Sat

Practice

One to three free-practice sessions where teams tune setup, gather tyre data, and learn the track. Nothing counts toward the result — it's all preparation.

Sessions FP1 · FP2 · FP360 min each
02 Saturday

Qualifying

A three-stage knockout that sets the starting order. The slowest cars are eliminated in Q1 and Q2; the top ten fight for pole position in Q3. Pure single-lap pace.

Format Q1 · Q2 · Q3Fastest = Pole
03 Sunday

The Race

Lights out. Drivers cover a set distance of about 305 km, managing tyres and fuel, diving for the pits, and racing wheel-to-wheel. Points go to the top ten finishers.

Distance ~305 kmMax 2 hours
Sprint Weekends

At six rounds each season the format changes: a short, flat-out Sprint race (about 100 km, no mandatory pit stop) is added with its own qualifying, handing extra points to the top eight and cramming even more racing into the weekend.

Engineering at the Limit

The Machines

An F1 car is a 798 kg carbon-fibre projectile built from tens of thousands of parts. Here's what makes it extraordinary.

~1000bhp

Hybrid Power Unit

A 1.6-litre turbocharged V6 works with electric energy-recovery motors that harvest power under braking and redeploy it for a punch of extra acceleration.

5× gravity

Downforce

Wings and floor generate so much aerodynamic grip that, at high speed, a Formula 1 car produces more than its own weight in downforce — enough to theoretically drive on a ceiling.

1000°C

Carbon Brakes

Carbon-carbon discs glow red as drivers scrub off huge speed in an instant, hauling the car from 300 to 100 km/h in around a second and a half under up to 6 G.

<2seconds

Pit Stops

A crew of twenty-plus changes all four wheels in the blink of an eye. The very fastest stops are done in under two seconds — faster than you can read this sentence.

5

Pirelli Tyres

One tyre supplier, several compounds. Teams must use at least two different dry compounds during a dry race, making tyre strategy a science of its own.

Survival Cell

Drivers sit inside a carbon monocoque built to survive enormous impacts, crowned by the titanium halo — a cockpit-protection hoop that has already saved lives.

2026

A New Era Begins

The 2026 season launches the biggest rule change in the sport's history — a ground-up reset of both the cars and the engines, designed to make racing closer and the sport more sustainable.

  • New power units with roughly half their output from electric power
  • 100% sustainable fuel replacing fossil-based petrol
  • Lighter, nimbler cars with active, moving aerodynamics
  • Audi arrives as a works team; Cadillac joins as the 11th team

Speak the Language

Trackside Lingo

A quick decoder for the phrases you'll hear on the broadcast and over the team radio.

Pole Position
First place on the starting grid, earned by setting the fastest lap in qualifying.
Box, Box
The radio call telling a driver to pit — from the German "boxenstopp." It's the cue to come in for tyres.
Undercut
Pitting before a rival for fresh tyres, then using the extra grip to leapfrog them once they stop.
Overcut
The opposite gamble — staying out longer on older tyres to build a gap and emerge ahead after the stops.
Dirty Air
Turbulent air behind a car that robs the following car of downforce, making it harder to stay close.
Slipstream
Tucking into the low-drag wake of the car ahead on a straight to gain speed — also called a tow.
DRS
Drag Reduction System: a driver-activated flap that opens the rear wing in designated zones to aid overtaking.
Parc Fermé
"Closed park" — the rules that lock car setup from qualifying to the race so teams can't make big changes.
Purple Sector
On the timing screens, purple marks the fastest time set by anyone; green means a driver's own personal best.
Formation Lap
The lap before the start, used to warm tyres and brakes before the cars line up for lights out.

and away we go.
